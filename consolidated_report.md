## Executive Summary

This Environmental Impact Assessment evaluates the development of CleanTech Park (Site A) and Bahar Industrial Estates (Site B) in western Singapore. The Project encompasses expansion of existing industrial and research facilities, infrastructure works including road realignment, utility installations, and construction of grade-separated wildlife crossings. The development sites comprise approximately 50 hectares of mixed habitats including native-dominated secondary forest, exotic-dominated secondary forest, abandoned kampung/plantation areas, scrubland, and managed landscapes.

Comprehensive baseline surveys conducted across nine taxonomic groups documented 127 species, of which 62 are of conservation significance. Notable threatened species include the Critically Endangered Sunda pangolin (Manis javanica), leopard cat (Prionailurus bengalensis), Barong's spotted emperor dragonfly (Heliaeschna crassa), and harlequin butterfly (Taxila haquinus haquinus). Flora surveys recorded four Critically Endangered plant species including Alchornea tiliifolia and Barringtonia racemosa, along with multiple Endangered and Vulnerable species. Site A retains 49.2% of abandoned kampung/plantation habitat while losing all forest stream habitat; Site B will lose 64.2% of abandoned kampung/plantation, 53.8% of exotic-dominated secondary forest, and the entirety of unmanaged soft-bank pond PB1. The sites provide critical stepping-stone habitat linking the Western Catchment Forest, Tengah Forest, Bukit Batok Nature Corridor, and Bukit Timah Nature Reserve.

Major construction impacts span multiple environmental parameters. Biodiversity impacts are assessed as Major prior to mitigation, affecting habitat integrity, forest edge formation, and causing mortality or displacement of 65 faunal species at Site B and 57 at Site A through loss of over 40% of suitable habitat. Hydrology and surface water quality impacts on Site B's natural streams D3 and D4 are Major under baseline scenarios, though reduced to Moderate if streams are retained. Air quality impacts from earthworks and trackout are Major at both sites due to proximity to Priority 1 ecological receptors. Airborne noise impacts range from Minor to Major, with 16-43% of study areas experiencing Major significance depending on time period and construction scenario. Ground-borne vibration from pipe jacking exceeds the 8.0 mm/s threshold for terrestrial burrow structural integrity, posing Major impacts to burrowing species including pangolins. Cumulative impacts are Major for regional habitat loss and ecological connectivity, with concurrent developments (HDB Tengah Town, LTA Tawas MRT, JTC Tengah High Volume Production facility, DTSS infrastructure) contributing to fragmentation of the western forest corridor.

The mitigation strategy adopts NParks' Biodiversity Impact Assessment hierarchy: avoidance through retention of conservation areas, minimisation through directional site clearance and Light Management Plans, restoration through infill planting and stream enhancement, and compensation through salvage/transplantation programs. With proposed mitigations, 1 Major habitat impact is reduced, 3 Moderate habitat impacts are reduced to Negligible-Minor; 46 Major flora impacts are reduced (though 3-7 species remain Moderate-Major as specimens are too large to transplant); and 28 Major fauna impacts and 134 Moderate fauna impacts are reduced, though 64-65 species retain Major significance due to irreversible habitat loss exceeding 40%. Dust impacts are reduced to Minor through water suppression and wheel-washing systems. Noise barriers achieve 17-52% reductions in Major impact areas at Site A depending on scenario. Ground-borne vibration from pipe jacking is reduced from Major to Moderate through daytime scheduling, avoidance of peak bird breeding season (March-July), and no night works after 7pm. Residual Major impacts remain for habitat loss at abandoned kampung/plantation areas, large transplant-ineligible fig species (Ficus spp.), and fauna dependent on bamboo clusters (50-75% loss) and forest streams (100% loss at Site A).

The Project implements an Environmental Management and Monitoring Plan (EMMP) featuring pre-felling fauna inspections valid for 7 days, daily fauna checks by Environmental Control Officers, monthly inspections by specialist ecologists, continuous real-time water quality monitoring with CCTV and Sediment and Turbidity Monitoring Systems at all discharge points, continuous air quality and noise monitoring at sensitive receptors, wildlife response protocols involving NParks and licensed contractors, 1.8-m perimeter hoarding embedded 30 cm into ground with wildlife exit points, and establishment of grade-separated wildlife crossings under Nanyang Avenue prior to site clearance. Monitoring data will be correlated with NEA regional datasets to contextualize short-term variations within long-term climatic trends. Ground-borne noise exceedances of up to 8 dB above the 47 dB LASmax threshold are predicted at CleanTech One from vibratory compaction, though these are likely masked by airborne construction noise in practice.

## Conclusion & Recommendations

The proposed development of CleanTech Park and Bahar Industrial Estates is environmentally acceptable subject to rigorous implementation of the comprehensive mitigation hierarchy and monitoring programs outlined in this EIA. Key residual risks include permanent loss of 49.2-64.2% of abandoned kampung/plantation habitat supporting Critically Endangered Sunda pangolins and leopard cats, irreversible loss of 50-75% of bamboo bat roosting sites, and the functional extirpation of stream-dependent species including the Critically Endangered harlequin butterfly and Vulnerable blue damselfly (Copera vittata) if forest streams are not retained. Cumulative habitat fragmentation across concurrent regional developments poses Major significance for ecological connectivity along the western forest corridor, with heightened roadkill risk for ground-dwelling megafauna during the construction phase when displaced animals navigate increasing traffic volumes along Nanyang Avenue and Jalan Bahar. Residual noise impacts remain Major for 16-24% of ecological study areas even with 6-m barriers, potentially causing chronic disturbance to breeding bird populations and nocturnal mammals.

From an objective standpoint, the development will result in the permanent conversion of approximately 50 hectares of secondary forest and regenerating habitats to industrial use, eliminating foraging and dispersal habitat for four Critically Endangered mammals (Sunda pangolin, leopard cat, Malayan porcupine, lesser mousedeer) and one Critically Endangered butterfly (harlequin). The 100% loss of forest streams at Site A will extirpate aquatic biodiversity from a 1.2-hectare freshwater system currently supporting native fish, turtles, and odonates. While salvage operations will attempt to rescue 29 flora species of conservation concern (including four Critically Endangered species), the success rate for large-specimen transplantation is inherently uncertain, and three Ficus species remain at Major risk due to their size. Bamboo bat populations face displacement from 50-75% of documented roost sites, with no guarantee that retained clusters possess sufficient carrying capacity. These ecological losses occur within a rapidly urbanizing western corridor where forest habitats now constitute less than 30% of the landscape, and where this Project eliminates critical stepping-stone habitat between Bukit Timah Nature Reserve and the Western Catchment.

**High nature / low development priority**: Recommend reducing Site A development footprint by 40% to retain the entirety of native-dominated secondary forest and all forest stream sections D1, D2, D3, and D4. Eliminate all construction works within 100 m of documented Sunda pangolin burrows and camera-trap detection points. Retain 100% of bamboo clusters through re-siting of northern infrastructure. Prohibit night works entirely to minimize disturbance to nocturnal species (pangolins, bats, owls, civets). Delay all construction until grade-separated wildlife crossings under Nanyang Avenue and Jalan Bahar are operational for a minimum 12-month habituation period. Establish 50-m vegetated buffers around all retained streams to protect riparian function. Abandon development of Site B entirely to preserve the 53.8% exotic-dominated secondary forest as regeneration habitat, maintain pond PB1 as critical habitat for fireflies (Pteroptyx valida) and aquatic herpetofauna, and retain streams D3/D4 as functional aquatic corridors. Implement 24-month pre-construction flora propagation programs to establish nursery stock from seed/cuttings of all conservation-significant plants prior to any site clearance, rather than relying on transplantation of mature specimens. Restrict all heavy vehicle movements to 0700-1800 hours and impose 15 km/hr speed limits on Nanyang Avenue and Jalan Bahar with wildlife warning signage and road mortality monitoring.

**Balanced priority**: Accept development of Site A with retention of 60% of native-dominated secondary forest in the northern and eastern conservation zones as proposed, but mandate full retention and enhancement of streams D3 and D4 at Site B rather than channelization or diversion. The Moderate residual impact on these Priority 2 streams with mitigation is acceptable only if stream retention includes 20-m riparian buffers, soft-bank morphology, native riparian planting (Cerbera odollam, Ficus microcarpa), and perpetual exclusion from future development. Accept loss of abandoned kampung/plantation habitat given its lower biodiversity value relative to native forest, but require pre-clearance rescue of all four Critically Endangered flora species (Alchornea tiliifolia, Barringtonia racemosa, Psychotria polycarpa, Leea angulata) with a minimum 70% transplant survival target verified through 24-month post-transplant monitoring. Require additional mitigation for bamboo bat impacts: retain minimum 60% of existing clusters and install 50 artificial bamboo roost structures in retained forest margins, monitored quarterly for two years to verify occupancy. Accept Major residual noise impacts in 16% of Site A study area (Scenario 1 daytime) as unavoidable, but require 8-m noise barriers (increase from proposed 6-m) at construction boundaries facing Bukit Batok Nature Park to achieve further 10-15% reduction in Major impact extent. Mandate cessation of all pipe jacking and excavation works during March-July bird breeding season as proposed, and prohibit any night works (post-1900h) within 200 m of forest edges. Install real-time wildlife monitoring cameras at all grade-separated crossings with weekly reporting to NParks to verify functional connectivity. The 50-75% bamboo cluster loss and 100% Site A stream loss remain the most problematic residual impacts, requiring compensation through off-site stream restoration (minimum 1:1 area ratio) and bamboo cluster establishment in adjacent Tengah Forest.

**High development / low nature priority**: Proceed with full Site A and Site B development footprints as proposed, accepting Major residual impacts for abandoned kampung/plantation habitat, bamboo clusters, and forest streams. Minimum mandatory safeguards: (1) Retain hoarding integrity and wildlife exit points to prevent construction site entrapment of pangolins and civets; (2) Execute Wildlife Response Plan for all trapped/injured animals to meet legal obligations under Wildlife Act; (3) Conduct pre-felling inspections as required to avoid prosecution for harming nesting birds or active pangolin burrows; (4) Install wheel-washing and dust suppression to meet NEA air quality standards and prevent regulatory enforcement; (5) Maintain real-time turbidity monitoring at discharge points to comply with NEA Trade Effluent Regulations and avoid Sewerage and Drainage Act violations; (6) Restrict pipe jacking PPV to <15 mm/s at CleanTech buildings to prevent cosmetic damage and contractual liability; (7) Implement night work restrictions (no works post-1900h for pipe jacking/excavation) to comply with NEA noise permit conditions; (8) Salvage minimum 50% of Critically Endangered flora specimens to demonstrate good-faith effort for NParks approval, even if transplant success is uncertain. Omit grade-separated wildlife crossings if road mortality rates remain below 10 pangolin carcasses per year (current baseline: occasional records). Omit infill planting in retained forest buffers to reduce costs. Implement Light Management Plan only in areas directly visible from Bukit Batok Nature Park to minimize light spill compliance costs. Accept 100% stream loss at Site A and channelization at Site B as necessary for drainage infrastructure. This approach meets statutory environmental requirements while maximizing developable land, though it forecloses future options for ecological restoration and accepts permanent extirpation of stream-dependent and forest-interior species from the site. Long-term reputational risk exists if regional pangolin population collapses due to cumulative habitat loss across western corridor developments.

## Key Findings

### Acoustic monitoring

- Bat acoustic surveys conducted using Song Meter Mini Bat recording ultrasonic calls between 18-192 kHz at 384 kHz sampling frequency. Recordings processed using Kaleidoscope v.4.5.4 with signal parameters: frequency 20-200 kHz, duration 2-500 ms, maximum inter-syllable gap 500 ms, minimum 2 pulses. Species identified based on call structures, peak frequency, minimum frequency and call duration *(CleanTech Park and Bahar Industrial Estates)*
  - **Significance**: Non-invasive methodology enables detection and identification of cryptic bat species including insectivorous bats that may not be detected through visual surveys alone

### Air quality correlation

- Correlation between site baseline and NEA's central area monitoring data will be useful for future monitoring as it provides reliable context for any future comparisons based on relation between the two datasets, acknowledging that short-term baselines fluctuate with seasonal variations in wind flow and direction *(CleanTech Park and Bahar)*
  - **Significance**: Establishes framework for interpreting short-term monitoring in context of long-term regional air quality trends

### Assessment Scope

- Study Area of 350m was considered for human health impacts during construction phase (from construction earthworks, trackout and excavation activities). For airborne noise, sensitive receptors are buildings within 150m from proposed construction worksites. For ground-borne noise and vibration, sensitive receptors were identified within 100m from site clearance area. *(Site A and Site B)*
  - **Significance**: Defines spatial extent of impact assessment for different environmental parameters

### Assessment criteria

- Due to lack of availability of criteria to assess ecological impacts arising from noise, a no worse-off than average baseline criteria proposed for adoption *(CleanTech Park and Bahar)*
  - **Significance**: Highlights absence of noise impact thresholds for ecological receptors in Singapore; pragmatic baseline approach developed

### Assessment methodology

- Ground-borne noise is reradiated noise from a building structure and is generally noticeable only in tranquil environments; it would typically be masked by airborne noise associated with surface construction activities.
  - **Significance**: This indicates that predicted ground-borne noise impacts may be less significant in practice than modelled values suggest, as airborne construction noise will likely dominate the acoustic environment.

### Assessment parameters

- Sensitive building receptors with levels below ground surface consider basement as the worst affected level, while other building receptors consider ground floor as the worst affected level for ground-borne noise and vibration.
  - **Significance**: This establishes the assessment approach for different building configurations to ensure worst-case impacts are captured.

### Bamboo bat habitat assessment

- GPS locations recorded for bamboo clusters < 3-m spread that may be important refugia for threatened bamboo bats. Roost emergence surveys conducted between 1830h and 2100h at bamboo clusters to detect bamboo bat presence, with recording of actively used bamboo slits and number of bats per internode *(CleanTech Park and Bahar Industrial Estates)*
  - **Significance**: Targets critical microhabitat for threatened bamboo bat species, providing detailed occupancy data

### Baseline change

- Compared to baseline study conducted in 2019 which only included area bounded by Jalan Bahar, Nanyang Avenue, and CleanTech Loop, habitat types found are similar. However, strip of scrubland/grassland vegetation recorded adjacent to CleanTech Heights in 2019 study has now been replaced with urban vegetation *(Site A)*
  - **Significance**: Recent habitat loss/conversion documented between 2019 and current assessment

### Baseline methodology

- Weekly groundwater gauging conducted for 12 weeks to obtain groundwater level fluctuation patterns. Rising head slug tests conducted on all eight wells to obtain site-specific hydraulic conductivity and derive groundwater flow velocity *(CleanTech Park and Bahar)*
  - **Significance**: Comprehensive hydrogeological characterization enables understanding of contaminant transport pathways

### Camera trap density

- Camera trap density of approximately 1 unit per 6.25 ha of forested area, determined with reference to the home range of Sunda pangolins (Manis javanica) *(CleanTech Park and Bahar Industrial Estates)*
  - **Significance**: Survey design specifically targets detection of threatened pangolin species and other wide-ranging mammals

### Concurrent development

- There are construction works related to PUB Deep Tunnel Sewerage System at southeast corner of Site B *(Site B)*
  - **Significance**: Concurrent infrastructure project may contribute to cumulative impacts

### Conservative assessment approach

- For conservative calculation of ground-borne noise, the entire transmission path is assumed to be soil/rock and no damping is applied. A correction of -10 dB is assumed for coupling loss into building structure for buildings with more than 10 storeys.
  - **Significance**: The assessment adopts conservative assumptions that may overestimate actual impacts, providing a margin of safety in the predictions.

### Construction Impact Context

- Site A is heavily disturbed by the construction site of the upcoming railway line, JW2 Tawas MRT station (part of the Jurong Regional Line) at the southern part of the Study Area. *(Site A)*
  - **Significance**: Pre-existing disturbance from concurrent infrastructure development affects baseline conditions

### Coordinate system

- Differential Global Positioning System (DGPS) with sub-meter accuracy used to record specimen locations using SVY21 plane coordinate system, the local datum used by Singapore Land Authority, rather than global datum *(CleanTech Park and Bahar Industrial Estates)*
  - **Significance**: Ensures maximum spatial accuracy and direct compatibility with Singapore national spatial datasets for planning integration

### Current infrastructure

- At present, completed developments include CleanTech One, CleanTech Two, CleanTech Three, LaunchPad@JID and SJ Campus housing offices, workshops and laboratories for CleanTech-related R&D activities. A*STAR has major presence with Advanced Remanufacturing and Technology Centre (ARTC), Singapore Institute of Manufacturing Technology (SIMTech), National Metrology Centre (NMC) and Additive Innovation Centre. Site features 5 ha Jurong Eco-Garden opened in 2014 now undergoing refurbishment. JW2 Tawas MRT station being constructed as part of Jurong Regional Line. Pedestrian overhead bridge completed in 2025 connecting to Block 864 Jurong West Street 81 *(Site A)*
  - **Significance**: Current developed infrastructure context including research facilities, green space, and new transport infrastructure

### Data Quality

- Initial molybdenum concentration of 1,154.6 µg/L (exceeding DIV of 300 µg/L) on 26-Jul-23 was not confirmed upon resampling on 27-Feb-24 (64 µg/L). The difference in results may be due to turbidity of the sample in which potential sediments were entrained in the sample that gave a positive bias on the molybdenum concentrations *(Site A - SAA-MW03)*
  - **Significance**: Highlights importance of sample handling protocols and resampling to verify contaminant exceedances, particularly for turbid groundwater samples

### Ecological connectivity monitoring

- Two camera traps deployed at existing culverts under Jalan Bahar to study movement of mammals across the road *(Jalan Bahar)*
  - **Significance**: Provides data on wildlife corridor functionality and road barrier effects for mammals moving between sites

### Groundwater-Surface Water Interaction

- Groundwater levels at all three monitoring wells (SAA-MW01: 8.448-8.576 m SHD; SAA-MW02: 14.130-14.177 m SHD; SAA-MW03: 12.308-12.624 m SHD) are generally higher than the watercourses' invert levels/riverbeds (7.5-10 m SHD for natural stream; 12.51-15 m SHD for man-made streams), which suggests that groundwater will most likely be feeding the streams *(Site A)*
  - **Significance**: Indicates potential for groundwater contamination to impact surface water quality through baseflow discharge

### Historical context

- Site A historically dominated by forested areas. Two existing developments (Jalan Bahar Clay Studios and Thow Kwang Pottery Jungle) existed since 1940s. Singapore had about 20 Dragon kilns from 1940s to 1970s. By 1990s, only Thow Kwang and Guan Huat Dragon Kilns were still in operation, both located in Jalan Bahar. Thow Kwang Dragon Kiln is oldest Dragon Kiln run by Tan family for three generations. Both kilns still in active operation *(Site A)*
  - **Significance**: Heritage features preserved on site representing Singapore's pottery industry history
- 1953 map showed village settlements near waterbodies in southern portion of Site B and now Bahar Industrial Estate. 1998 map indicated village settlements no longer exist. Google Earth imagery indicates areas were re-vegetated. Western Catchment Area (WWCA) is one of remaining largest undeveloped mangroves, coastal and wetland areas in Singapore containing four reservoirs developed in 1980s *(Site B)*
  - **Significance**: Historical land use transition from settlements to re-vegetated areas, and regional context of important remaining wetland/mangrove areas

### Impact significance framework

- Impact significance determined using risk-based matrix combining consequence (imperceptible to high) and likelihood (unlikely/remote to highly likely/continuous). Negligible and minor impacts deemed 'Insignificant' requiring no additional management. Moderate impacts deemed 'Relatively Significant' and major impacts deemed 'Significant', both requiring adoption of management or mitigation measures
  - **Significance**: Defines threshold for when impacts require mitigation

### Landscape Context

- Site A contains two existing developments/structures (Jalan Bahar Clay Studios and Thow Kwang Pottery Jungle) that have been present since the 1940s and represent historical and cultural amenity in the Study Area. These are classified as Priority 2 landscape receptors with medium quality, medium rarity, and medium ability to accommodate change. *(Site A)*
  - **Significance**: Cultural facilities represent heritage assets requiring consideration in impact assessment

### Landscape context

- Singapore has warmed by average of 0.25°C per decade since 1948, with visible rapid increase after mid-1970s possibly due to rapid economic development and urbanization. Eight of ten warmest years recorded occurred in 21st century, all ten occurred after 1997. Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect present with maximum temperature difference of 4.01°C observed between well-planted areas (Lim Chu Kang) and Central Business District *(Singapore)*
  - **Significance**: Regional climate change context and importance of green areas in mitigating urban heat
- Rainfall is higher over northern and western parts of Singapore and decreases towards eastern part. Central Catchment possibly receives maximum rainfall. Overall upward trend in total annual rainfall at rates ranging from 0.3 mm/year to 15.3 mm/year during 1980-2022, except areas near Queenstown climate station in south *(Singapore)*
  - **Significance**: Regional rainfall patterns showing spatial variation and increasing trends relevant to drainage and flood risk

### Methodological approach

- Ground-borne noise levels are usually derived from ground-borne vibration levels, and ground-borne noise level has to be higher than the airborne noise from the noise source to be measurable, thus no baseline monitoring for ground-borne noise was carried out
  - **Significance**: Explains rationale for not conducting ground-borne noise baseline monitoring
- No human receptor was chosen for baseline monitoring in Site B as the receptors identified are close to the receptors in the Site A study area; thus, baseline monitoring for human receptors in Site A was deemed to be sufficient to represent the baseline vibration levels of both Site A and Site B *(Site B)*
  - **Significance**: Justifies spatial efficiency in baseline monitoring design across both sites
- Baseline vibration monitoring conducted using Svantek 958A with SV207B tri-axial accelerometer. Monitoring equipment set to record Peak Particle Velocity PPV data at 1-minute intervals for at least three (3) days
  - **Significance**: Establishes technical specifications and duration for vibration monitoring

### Methodological limitation

- Dutch Intervention Values were developed to assess acceptability of impacted soil and groundwater at housing estates in Netherlands based on local Dutch ecotoxicology and soil conditions (10% organic clay or 25% clay), without reference to commercial/industrial or similar land uses in Singapore. Exceedances should not necessarily be interpreted as conclusive regarding need for remediation *(CleanTech Park and Bahar)*
  - **Significance**: Indicates potential limitations in applying international standards to Singapore context; conservative approach adopted

### Methodology

- Eight boreholes drilled to 6m depth below ground surface using manual hand auger, with two soil samples per borehole selected for laboratory analysis - one approximately within invert level of waterbodies to assess sediment impact on freshwater ecology, and one at depths with higher PID/staining/odour *(CleanTech Park and Bahar)*
  - **Significance**: Establishes systematic approach to identifying potential contamination relevant to ecological receptors

### Mitigation hierarchy framework

- Mitigation follows NParks Biodiversity Impact Assessment (BIA) Guidelines hierarchy: (1) Avoid, (2) Minimise, (3) Remedy/Repair/Restore, (4) Compensation/Offset. Acceptable level is reduction of major impact to moderate after mitigation. For moderate impacts, emphasis on demonstrating impact reduced to as low as reasonably practicable, considering cost-effectiveness and diminishing returns
  - **Significance**: Establishes structured approach to mitigation aligned with Singapore biodiversity guidelines

### Multi-height monitoring

- For high rise residential sensitive receptors, noise monitoring conducted at different floor heights (ground, mid-level 7 storey, high level 14 storey at HDB Block 851) to capture terrain variation and its impact on noise levels *(Site A - CleanTech Park)*
  - **Significance**: Acknowledges vertical variation in noise exposure for high-rise receptors, providing more comprehensive baseline

### Other

- Temperature and humidity meters (Kestrel DROP D3) and light meters (HOBO Pendant MX Temp/Light MX2202 Logger) deployed at 0 m, 50 m, and 100 m from Development Boundary to inform construction or operational phase mitigation measures
  - **Significance**: Microclimate and light monitoring designed to support adaptive mitigation strategies

### Project Scale

- The Project is expected to generate >100,000 tonnes of spoil within both Site A and Site B. About >10 heavy vehicles/machineries are expected to be operating on site at any time throughout construction. Number of outward truck movements expected to be around >50 HDVs per day for both Site A and Site B, with <100m unpaved roads involved. *(Site A and Site B)*
  - **Significance**: Defines scale of construction activity and potential for dust/noise/vibration impacts

### Receptor Classification

- All watercourses within the Study Area are classified as Priority 1 human sensitive receptors for water quality, as the waters will flow into reservoirs which will be used for drinking water purposes. For hydrology, watercourses are classified as Priority 2 human sensitive receptors, as the waters may cause flooding in the vicinity. *(Site A and Site B)*
  - **Significance**: Establishes receptor sensitivity hierarchy for water-related impacts

### Receptor Proximity

- Notable sensitive receptors to Site A include 71 Nanyang Crescent (~20m from worksite), Nanyang Meadows Staff Housing (98, 100 Nanyang Crescent, ~30m), and NTU Sports Hall (110 Nanyang Crescent, ~50m). Site A earthworks classified as Priority 1 sensitivity; construction as Priority 2; trackout as Priority 1. *(Site A)*
  - **Significance**: Very close proximity of educational and residential receptors drives high sensitivity classification
- Notable sensitive receptor to Site B is the residential area at Westville, approximately 160m away from the worksite. Site B earthworks classified as Priority 2 sensitivity; trackout as Priority 1. *(Site B)*
  - **Significance**: Greater distance to receptors results in lower sensitivity classification for some impact pathways

### Receptor identification

- Air quality monitoring locations selected based on identification of Air Sensitive Receptors (ASRs) including schools, childcare facilities, residences, flora and habitats of high ecological value nearest to study area, avoiding areas with ongoing construction *(CleanTech Park and Bahar)*
  - **Significance**: Systematic approach ensures baseline captures conditions at most vulnerable receptors

### Risk Threshold

- Soil baseline analytical results at Site B reported small, localised concentrations of copper in soil (SAB-BH02) above its Dutch Intervention Value (DIV), hence potential unacceptable risk to human health may exist. *(Site B)*
  - **Significance**: Pre-existing soil contamination above health-based threshold requiring management

### Risk threshold

- Ground-borne vibration threshold for ecological receptors: Lower bound of baseline within ecological area is 0.03 mm/s PPV. Negligible impact: < 0.06 mm/s; Low impact: 0.06 - < 1.2 mm/s; Medium impact: 1.2 - < 5.0 mm/s; High impact: ≥ 5.0 mm/s *(Ecological areas)*
  - **Significance**: Establishes critical vibration thresholds for ecological receptors based on baseline conditions, with no relevant national or international standards available
- Ground-borne vibration for human receptors Priority 2 (residential): All intensity levels (Negligible to High) set at < PPV 0.30 mm/s
  - **Significance**: Sets conservative threshold for residential receptors based on British Standard BS 5228-2:2009+A1:2014
- Ground-borne vibration for human receptors Priority 3 (commercial): Negligible < 1.0 mm/s; Low 1.0 - < 10.0 mm/s; Medium ≥ 10.0 - < 15.0 mm/s. PPV greater than 15 mm/s will lead to cosmetic damage, thus not acceptable
  - **Significance**: Establishes damage threshold at 15 mm/s PPV for buildings, preventing structural impacts

### Sensitive Equipment

- CleanTech Two and Three have already been structurally designed with sufficient rigidity to satisfy vibration criteria (e.g. deeper beams, more rigid pile foundations) without the need for further mitigation strategies. Thus, assessment for vibration sensitive equipment has been excluded from this Report. *(Site A)*
  - **Significance**: Pre-emptive structural design eliminates need for vibration impact mitigation on sensitive equipment

### Species documentation

- GPS coordinates recorded for all plants of conservation significance using Garmin GPSMap 64s (accuracy ± 4 m). For clusters of conservation species within 5 m of each other, approximate center coordinates marked *(CleanTech Park and Bahar Industrial Estates)*
  - **Significance**: Enables precise spatial mapping of threatened flora for impact avoidance and compensation planning

### Survey coverage

- Vegetation plot sampling conducted at one 20 × 20 m plot per 5 ha of spontaneous vegetation (forested area) accessible on foot, excluding steep terrain areas. Data used to visualize differences among vegetation types using non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) ordination and understand forest successional stages to inform habitat enhancement recommendations *(CleanTech Park and Bahar Industrial Estates)*
  - **Significance**: Quantitative vegetation sampling provides statistical basis for characterizing forest structure, composition, and successional status to guide evidence-based restoration planning

### Survey methodology

- Comprehensive multi-taxa survey approach covering nine taxonomic groups: butterflies, odonates, aculeate hymenopterans, herpetofauna, birds, non-volant mammals, bats, freshwater fauna (fish, decapod crustaceans, molluscs), and spiders. Each survey conducted twice with at least one week interval between cycles. *(CleanTech Park and Bahar Industrial Estates)*
  - **Significance**: Establishes robust baseline biodiversity data through standardized methodology across all taxa with temporal replication

### Survey scope

- Roadkill surveys carried out along Nanyang Avenue and Jalan Bahar between 0700h and 1000h, with additional recording of carcasses seen outside survey window *(Nanyang Avenue and Jalan Bahar)*
  - **Significance**: Quantifies wildlife-vehicle collision mortality along key roads bisecting or adjacent to study sites

### Survey timing

- All bird surveys were carried out during the peak bird migratory season in Singapore, which is between September and February *(CleanTech Park and Bahar Industrial Estates)*
  - **Significance**: Ensures capture of both resident and migratory bird species for complete avifaunal assessment

### Temporal Trends

- The stabilised water level elevation in monitoring wells SAB-MW01 and SAB-MW04 were observed to be trending downwards after the 7th gauging event (09 Sep 2023 - 15 Sep 2023), while other monitoring wells showed limited variations over the 12 gauging events *(Site B)*
  - **Significance**: Suggests potential localized changes in groundwater recharge or extraction affecting specific monitoring locations

### Vibration cosmetic damage threshold

- All predicted ground-borne vibration levels from construction activities are below the cosmetic damage threshold of 15 mm/s PPV, with maximum predicted levels ranging from 1 to 9 mm/s at both sites. *(CleanTech Park (Site A) and Bahar Industrial Estates (Site B))*
  - **Significance**: This demonstrates that construction vibration will not cause structural or cosmetic damage to nearby buildings, although minor impacts on human comfort may occur.

### Water quality sampling strategy

- Fifteen water quality monitoring locations established across both sites, with samples collected during both dry-weather conditions (after continuous 48-hour no-rain period) and wet-weather conditions (within 3 hours after rainfall event >10mm). Two dry weather and one wet weather samples collected from majority of stations *(CleanTech Park and Bahar Industrial Estates)*
  - **Significance**: Comprehensive baseline captures temporal variation and event-driven changes in surface water quality across multiple watercourses
